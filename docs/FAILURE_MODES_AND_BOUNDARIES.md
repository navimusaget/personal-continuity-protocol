# Failure Modes and Boundaries

**Boundary note - not legal advice.**

A serious continuity protocol must say where it fails.

PCP does not protect against every form of power. It does not guarantee that a court will accept a package, that a bank will pay, that a state will cooperate, that a platform will obey, or that a family will not fight.

## PCP does not

- override lawful court orders;
- bypass KYC/AML, sanctions or tax obligations;
- replace a legally valid will, trust, contract or court process;
- determine inheritance by itself;
- recover continuity after total loss of all anchors, witnesses, records and institutional traces;
- prevent all collusion among state, corporate, family, professional and platform actors;
- make an incompetent steward competent;
- turn an old instruction into current will without freshness or legal pathway;
- turn silence into proof of life, death, consent or authority;
- guarantee ordinary users execute the ceremony correctly without help.

## PCP is strongest against

- silence;
- confusion;
- mistaken transfer;
- operational rupture;
- lost interfaces;
- weak evidence;
- false first-mover advantage;
- avoidable disputes;
- accidental erasure.

## PCP is weakest against

- total coercive power;
- lawful court orders;
- captured institutions;
- bad agents;
- total loss of all anchors;
- human failure at setup.

## Core rule

Proof is not enforcement.

Proof makes refusal contestable.
